WORK ABOUT RESUME CONTACT

Modernizing a dated booking experience to recover falling conversions

GolfPac Travel offers custom golf vacation packages across the US. The existing site had an early-2000s look and feel, and the client's core concern was that this outdated presentation was actively costing them conversions — users landing on the site weren't trusting or engaging with it the way a modern travel platform would expect.

Role

Freelance Product Designer & Frontend Developer, working directly in the AngularJS front-end alongside the design work.

Duration

August 2024 to September 2025.

Client

GolfPac Travel, a US golf vacation package provider spanning quote requests, self-serve trip building, and instant packages.

The site's visual design and interaction patterns hadn't kept pace with modern booking experiences, and it wasn't obvious how to move from browsing to requesting a quote.

What the client was seeing

  • An early-2000s look and feel that read as untrustworthy to modern travel shoppers
  • Falling conversion rates the client attributed directly to the outdated presentation
  • No clear first action — three planning paths competed for attention with no obvious entry point
  • A booking flow that didn't guide users from "interested" to "requesting a quote"

The brief was direct: modernize the visual design, and make sure every change served a real usability purpose rather than just a cosmetic refresh — improve how users could explore options and move through the site with confidence.

Before and after the redesign

Before

An early-2000s layout with three competing entry points and no clear first action, on the previous booking site the client asked GolfPac to move away from.

No screenshot of the legacy site is shown here to keep this case study focused on GolfPac's own product rather than a third-party site.

Three decisions carried most of the work.

One clear entry point

Three competing calls to action became a single tabbed choice — Request a Quote, Design Your Own, or Instant Packages — so the first decision a visitor has to make is obvious instead of a guess.

Progressive, sectioned flow

The planning process breaks into clear steps — trip details, group details, preferences and add-ons, then contact info — each in its own section, so users always know where they are and what comes next.

Closing the loop

The confirmation step restates the request, sets a 24 to 48 hour response-time expectation, and lists exactly what happens next — so users leave the flow knowing what to expect instead of wondering if it worked.

Alongside the flow changes, the visual language was rebuilt from the ground up: modern layout, imagery treatment, and typography replaced the dated early-2000s presentation, and small icons were added next to key labels throughout — trip details, lodging, golf preferences — to improve scannability without adding to the reading load.

I worked directly in the AngularJS front-end alongside the design work, implementing the new flow structure rather than handing off static screens.

Clearer at every step, from stakeholders' own read of it.

1 entry point Down from three competing CTAs to one tabbed choice
4 sectioned steps Trip details, group details, preferences, contact — each its own step
24–48 hr Response-time expectation set clearly at confirmation

Stakeholders reported that the redesigned flow was noticeably clearer than the previous version — users could navigate the site and understand their options without confusion, and the more prominent CTAs made the intended next step obvious at every stage. I don't have analytics access as a freelancer, so I'm reporting that as stakeholder feedback rather than a conversion number I can't verify.
