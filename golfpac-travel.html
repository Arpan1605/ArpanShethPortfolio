01 Travel Platform Case Study

Modernizing a dated golf travel platform to fix a broken decision-making journey

Industry: Travel & Hospitality Duration: Aug 2024 – Jan 2025 (design) · Feb 2025 – Sep 2025 (frontend development) Role: Product Designer & Frontend Developer Method: Stakeholder workshops, wireframing, usability heuristics

An earlier exploration pass — the visual language was refined further through the high-fidelity stage below.

🔎 Overview

Context. GolfPac Travel lets golfers book complete multi-day trips across US destinations — golf rounds, lodging/resort stays, and rental cars — all in one place. The site's early-2000s look and feel didn't reflect the scope of what it actually offered: golfers planning a genuinely complex, multi-part trip were landing on a page that felt more like a basic tee-time booker than a full trip-builder, and were met with three different, competing ways to start planning with no indication of which one to use.

Business goal. Modernize the visual design and fix the underlying decision-making flow so the site actually reflected the breadth of what GolfPac offers — a complete trip (golf, stay, transport), not just a round of golf — while making every design change earn its place by solving a real usability problem.

Results

  • ✅ Stakeholders confirmed the new flow was noticeably clearer than the old site
  • ✅ Prominent, unambiguous CTAs at every step of the journey

Exact conversion/analytics data wasn't available to me as an external freelancer — see the messy bits below.

🤷‍♀️ The User & Problem

Golfers planning a considered, multi-part purchase — with no clear way to start.

User group. Golfers planning multi-day golf trips in the US, typically booking for themselves and a small group, comparing multiple destinations before committing.

Illustrative persona, built from stakeholder input rather than formal user interviews.

Michael R.

52 years, Frequent Golf Traveler

Decisive Value-conscious Time-pressed Loyal

"I want to book my trip in one sitting. If I have to guess which button starts the process, I've already lost trust in the company handling my vacation."

Goals

  • Compare multiple golf destinations quickly and confidently.
  • Get a transparent, all-in price before committing.
  • Book an entire multi-day trip without needing to call anyone.

Pain Points & Objections

  • Three different "start planning" buttons with no explanation of the difference.
  • Outdated design undermines trust for such a high-cost, considered purchase.

Expectations & Needs

  • A single, obvious way to start planning a trip.
  • Clear pricing shown upfront, not hidden behind a quote request.
  • A site that feels current and trustworthy for the money involved.

Ability to Use Technology

  • Comfortable booking travel online end-to-end.
  • Prefers self-service but expects a phone fallback to be available if something is unclear.
  • Low patience for multi-step forms with unclear progress.

The problem. Without a clear entry point, users were left to guess which planning method matched their needs — and an outdated visual design compounded the trust problem for what's a significant, considered purchase covering golf, lodging, and transport together.

⚒️ My Approach

Workshops to lock the flow, then wireframes, then a full visual rebuild.

I ran stakeholder workshops with the GolfPac team over roughly six months to understand where the existing flow broke down, then worked through structural black-and-white wireframes before any visual design began — locking the flow logic first. Once the design was validated, I moved into the frontend development phase, implementing the redesign directly in AngularJS.

Key insights

  • Three competing CTAs on the homepage created decision paralysis before a user even started planning
  • The visual design itself was actively undermining trust for an expensive, considered purchase
  • Each of the three planning paths served a genuinely different user intent (guided help, self-serve building, instant booking) — the fix wasn't picking one, it was making the choice between them clear

Wireframes: locking the flow logic first.

Before moving to visual design, I mapped the full GolfPac experience in low-fidelity wireframes — entry and account flows, the three planning paths, supporting content pages, and the full checkout sequence.

High-fidelity design: GolfPac's brand, in full.

With structure validated, I moved to full visual design — establishing GolfPac's brand identity (cyan accent, clean white surfaces, real course and destination photography) across the complete experience.

🚨 The Messy Bits

What went wrong, and how I fixed it.

Problem 1

As a freelancer without direct access to GolfPac's analytics, I couldn't validate my design decisions against real conversion data mid-project — only stakeholder-reported qualitative feedback.

How I handled it: Leaned more heavily on usability heuristics and stakeholder workshops to compensate, and was upfront with the client about the limits of what I could measure from outside their systems.

Problem 2

Stakeholders initially wanted the Destinations page to support multi-select checkboxes — letting users tick several locations at once and search across all of them simultaneously. From a product perspective it seemed like it gave users more power, but from a UX standpoint it worked against how people actually plan a trip like this: the way users book flights, stays, or trips elsewhere (Airbnb being the obvious reference point) is linear — pick one destination, see what's available, then decide — not comparing multiple locations' entire inventories at once. Layering that on top of the existing three competing planning paths risked compounding the exact decision-paralysis problem this redesign was meant to fix.

How I handled it: Rather than simply pushing back with an opinion, I put together multiple concrete scenarios showing how a multi-select search would actually play out for a user — comparing it side by side with a linear, single-destination flow — to make the trade-off tangible rather than abstract. Walking stakeholders through real cases made the argument land in a way that a general "this is more usable" claim wouldn't have: sometimes giving users more options up front doesn't make their life easier, it makes the decision harder. The team agreed to keep destination selection linear.

✅ The Outcome

Clearer at every step, from stakeholders' own read of it.

1 entry point Down from three competing CTAs to one tabbed choice
2-stage process Wireframes locked the flow before any visual design began
4-step checkout Traveler → Review → Payment → Confirm, tracked at every step

The redesigned flow replaced three competing, confusing entry points with one clear guided choice, backed by a modernized visual design built to earn trust for a high-cost, multi-part purchase. Stakeholders reported the new flow was significantly easier to navigate, with the intended next step obvious at every stage.

↪️ What I Would Do Differently Next Time

Push earlier and harder for direct access to analytics or usage data, even as an external freelancer — qualitative stakeholder feedback is valuable, but pairing it with real usage numbers would have let me validate (or challenge) my own design decisions more rigorously.
