WORK ABOUT RESUME CONTACT

Modernizing a dated booking experience to recover falling conversions

GolfPac Travel offers custom golf vacation packages across the US. The existing site had an early-2000s look and feel, and the client's core concern was that this outdated presentation was actively costing them conversions — users landing on the site weren't trusting or engaging with it the way a modern travel platform would expect.

Role

Freelance Product Designer & Frontend Developer, working directly in the AngularJS front-end alongside the design work.

Duration

August 2024 to September 2025.

Client

GolfPac Travel, a US golf vacation package provider spanning quote requests, self-serve trip building, and instant packages.

An earlier exploration pass — the visual language was refined further through the high-fidelity stage below.

The site's visual design and interaction patterns hadn't kept pace with modern booking experiences, and the booking flow itself didn't clearly guide users toward action.

What the client was seeing

  • An early-2000s look and feel that read as untrustworthy to modern travel shoppers
  • Falling conversion rates the client attributed directly to the outdated presentation
  • Three separate planning paths competing for attention on the homepage with no shared structure, making it unclear which one to use
  • A booking flow that didn't clearly guide users toward action beyond the aesthetics

The client's brief was direct: modernize the visual design, and make sure every change served a real usability purpose rather than just a cosmetic refresh — improve how users could explore options and move through the site with confidence.

Wireframes first, then a full visual rebuild.

I worked through this in two stages: structural black-and-white wireframes first, to lock down flow logic before any visual design began, followed by full high-fidelity design once the structure was validated.

Wireframes: locking the flow logic first.

Before moving to visual design, I mapped the full GolfPac experience in low-fidelity wireframes — entry and account flows, the three planning paths, supporting content pages, and the full checkout sequence.

High-fidelity design: GolfPac's brand, in full.

With structure validated, I moved to full visual design — establishing GolfPac's brand identity (cyan accent, clean white surfaces, real photography) across the complete experience. The photo areas below are shown as color placeholders rather than the real course and destination photography, since the original image files weren't available to me directly — everything else (copy, structure, pricing, badges) reflects the real design.

Clearer at every step, from stakeholders' own read of it.

1 entry point Down from three competing CTAs to one tabbed choice
2-stage process Wireframes locked the flow before any visual design began
4-step checkout Traveler → Review → Payment → Confirm, tracked at every step

Stakeholders reported that the redesigned flow was noticeably clearer than the previous version — users could navigate the site and understand their options without confusion, and the more prominent CTAs made the intended next step obvious at every stage. I don't have analytics access as a freelancer, so I'm reporting that as stakeholder feedback rather than a conversion number I can't verify.
